Ask an LLM "What's 17 × 24?" and it might blurt out "398" (wrong). Ask it to "think step by step" and it writes "17 × 20 = 340, 17 × 4 = 68, 340 + 68 = 408" (correct). The model is the same. The weights are identical. The only difference: you told it to **show its work**.

This is **Chain of Thought (CoT)** prompting — and it is one of the most consequential discoveries in prompt engineering.

## Why Direct Answers Fail

LLMs generate text **one token at a time**, each token conditioned on everything that came before. When you ask for a direct answer, the model must compress the entire reasoning process into its internal hidden states — no scratchpad, no working memory, no ability to verify intermediate steps.

For simple factual recall ("What's the capital of France?"), this works fine. For multi-step reasoning — math, logic, code analysis, spatial reasoning — it often doesn't. The model essentially has to "think without writing," and errors compound silently.

## How Chain of Thought Works

When the model writes intermediate reasoning steps, **each step becomes part of the context for generating the next step**. The visible text acts as external working memory:

```
Direct: Question → [hidden reasoning] → Final Answer
CoT:    Question → Step 1 → Step 2 → Step 3 → Final Answer
                   ↑ context for Step 2
                            ↑ context for Step 3
                                       ↑ context for Answer
```

Each intermediate step constrains the solution space. If Step 1 produces "3 × $2 = $6", Step 2 is now highly likely to subtract from the correct base rather than an invented number. Errors become visible and self-correctable.

## When CoT Helps Most

Chain of thought produces the largest accuracy gains on tasks that require:

- **Multi-step arithmetic**: Word problems with multiple operations
- **Logical deduction**: Syllogisms, constraint satisfaction, puzzles
- **Code reasoning**: Tracing execution paths, identifying edge cases
- **Spatial/temporal reasoning**: Tracking state changes through sequences of actions
- **Planning**: Breaking a complex task into ordered subtasks

For simple factual recall or pattern matching, CoT adds tokens without improving accuracy.

## The Cost Trade-Off

Chain of thought is not free:

- **More tokens**: CoT responses can be 3-10x longer than direct answers, increasing both latency and cost
- **Context consumption**: The reasoning steps consume context window space that could hold other information
- **Not always necessary**: For simple tasks, CoT is overhead without benefit

The practical approach: **use CoT for complex reasoning tasks, skip it for simple retrieval and classification.**

## Variants of Chain of Thought

**Zero-shot CoT**: Simply appending "Let's think step by step" to any prompt. Surprisingly effective — discovered by [Kojima et al. (2022)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.11916).

**Few-shot CoT**: Providing examples that include reasoning steps. The model mimics the demonstrated reasoning pattern.

**Self-consistency**: Running CoT multiple times with temperature > 0 and taking the majority answer. Reduces variance at the cost of multiple API calls.

**Tree of Thought**: Exploring multiple reasoning paths in parallel and selecting the most promising branch. More complex but more powerful for hard problems.

## Key Takeaways

- Chain of thought prompting tells the model to reason out loud before answering
- The written reasoning acts as external working memory, constraining each step based on the previous one
- CoT dramatically improves accuracy on multi-step reasoning tasks (math, logic, code, spatial)
- It costs more tokens and time — use it selectively for complex problems
- "Let's think step by step" is a zero-shot CoT trigger that requires no examples
- The model's knowledge is unchanged — CoT just gives it a scratchpad to use that knowledge more effectively

Reasoning is not decoration. It is computation. When you tell the model to think step by step, you are literally giving it more compute — each token of reasoning is an additional forward pass through the model's weights.
