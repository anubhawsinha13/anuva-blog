Every time you interact with ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini, an invisible parameter shapes whether you get a conservative, predictable response or a creative, unpredictable one. That parameter is temperature, and understanding it separates engineers who treat LLMs as black boxes from those who engineer precise AI behaviours.

Temperature is not merely a tuning knob. It is a fundamental control mechanism that directly manipulates the probability distribution of next-token predictions. Set it wrong, and your code generator produces syntactically broken functions. Set it right, and the same model transitions from producing boilerplate to exploring novel algorithmic approaches.

## What Temperature Actually Does Under the Hood

Temperature scales the logit values before applying softmax during token selection. At temperature 1.0, the model uses raw probability distributions from its final layer. At 0.0, it performs greedy decoding — always selecting the highest-probability token. At 2.0, it flattens the distribution, making low-probability tokens nearly as likely as high-probability ones.

```python
import numpy as np

def apply_temperature(logits, temperature):
    if temperature == 0:
        probs = np.zeros_like(logits)
        probs[np.argmax(logits)] = 1.0
        return probs
    scaled_logits = logits / temperature
    exp_logits = np.exp(scaled_logits - np.max(scaled_logits))
    return exp_logits / np.sum(exp_logits)

logits = np.array([2.5, 2.0, 1.5, 0.5])

print("T=0.3:", apply_temperature(logits, 0.3))
# [0.94, 0.05, 0.01, 0.00] — sharply peaked

print("T=1.0:", apply_temperature(logits, 1.0))
# [0.51, 0.31, 0.14, 0.04] — balanced

print("T=2.0:", apply_temperature(logits, 2.0))
# [0.36, 0.28, 0.22, 0.14] — nearly flat
```

[OpenAI's Chat Completions API](https://platform.openai.com/docs/api-reference/chat) supports temperatures from 0.0 to 2.0, defaulting to 1.0. [Anthropic's Messages API](https://docs.anthropic.com/en/api/messages) caps at 1.0. [Google's Gemini API](https://ai.google.dev/gemini-api/docs) mirrors OpenAI's 0–2 range. These defaults represent each vendor's position on the creativity-reliability spectrum for general-purpose use.

## The Low Temperature Zone: Consistency Over Creativity

Low temperature settings (0.0–0.5) excel when variance is a bug, not a feature. Classification tasks are the canonical use case. When performing sentiment analysis on customer feedback, you need the model to classify "This product is terrible" identically across thousands of API calls — not produce different labels based on sampling randomness.

Code generation for strongly-typed languages benefits from the same logic. When completing a Java method signature or a SQL query, you want the statistically most likely valid syntax, not an experimental variation that might introduce a subtle type error.

| Temperature | Prompt: "Write a function to validate email" | Behaviour |
|-------------|----------------------------------------------|-----------|
| 0.0 | Identical output every run: standard regex with common rules | Deterministic — always highest-probability tokens |
| 0.3 | Occasionally varies variable names, core logic identical | Minimal variation within safe choices |
| 0.5 | May choose between regex and string-method implementations | Explores top probable paths |

The downside is predictability. At temperature 0.0, models fall into repetitive sentence structures. Customer-facing chatbots at this setting feel scripted — a quality drop users notice quickly.

## The Medium Temperature Sweet Spot

Most production systems operate between 0.5 and 0.9, treating temperature as a balancing mechanism between consistency and natural variation. This zone produces outputs that feel human-authored while maintaining enough determinism for quality control.

Conversational AI benefits most from this range. The model still grounds outputs in high-probability sequences but introduces enough randomness to avoid sounding mechanical across multi-turn conversations. Technical documentation generation also suits this range — consistent terminology with enough phrasing variation to avoid the "AI-generated" feel where every paragraph opens with "It is important to note that."

The practical challenge: optimal medium temperature varies by task and model. Temperature 0.7 may produce excellent chatbot responses with Claude but overly verbose technical writing with GPT-4. Test empirically against your specific use case rather than trusting defaults.

## The High Temperature Zone: Creativity at the Cost of Reliability

High temperature settings (1.0–2.0) transform LLMs from conservative assistants into creative collaborators willing to explore unusual probability spaces. This zone enables brainstorming, creative writing, and exploratory research where novel combinations matter more than reliability.

At temperature 1.5, a model asked to "suggest unconventional database designs for a real-time chat application" might propose CRDTs or operational transformation — approaches statistically less common than "use PostgreSQL with WebSockets" but potentially more interesting for the specific context.

The creativity boost carries substantial risks. High temperatures increase hallucination rates, produce syntactically invalid code, and generate logically inconsistent outputs. The model samples from the long tail of its training distribution, where patterns are sparse and learned associations are weaker. Inference stability degrades sharply above 1.5 — each high-variance token selection creates a context for subsequent high-variance selections, compounding divergence from high-probability paths.

## Dynamic Temperature: Matching Setting to Phase

Sophisticated applications do not use static temperature. They adjust based on task phase and output requirements.

```python
class AdaptiveResearchAssistant:
    def research_query(self, question):
        # Phase 1: Decompose — needs determinism
        subquestions = self.llm(question, temperature=0.3)

        # Phase 2: Gather — balance accuracy with completeness
        gathered = [self.llm(q, temperature=0.7) for q in subquestions]

        # Phase 3: Synthesise — encourage creative connections
        synthesis = self.llm(str(gathered), temperature=1.2)

        # Phase 4: Format — consistent structure and tone
        return self.llm(synthesis, temperature=0.4)
```

Logical decomposition benefits from determinism. Synthesis benefits from exploration. Formatting benefits from consistency. Different cognitive tasks have different temperature optima — the pipeline expresses this explicitly rather than using a single setting throughout.

Customer service bots use the same pattern: temperature 0.3 for policy lookups, 0.7 for general conversation, 1.0 for product recommendations that benefit from variety.

## Practical Selection Framework

**Temperature 0.0** — Legal document analysis, medical diagnosis assistance, financial calculations, reproducible debugging. Complete elimination of sampling randomness matters more than any quality improvements from exploration.

**Temperature 0.1–0.3** — Structured data extraction, classification, code completion in strongly-typed languages. Minimal variance, tolerant of functionally equivalent variations.

**Temperature 0.5–0.8** — Conversational AI, technical writing, general code generation. Human-feeling outputs without sacrificing reliability. This is where most production systems land.

**Temperature 1.0–1.5** — Brainstorming, creative writing, exploratory ideation. Outputs you will manually review. The value from diversity outweighs the cost of occasional coherence failures.

**Above 1.5** — Avoid unless you have a specific reason and can afford extensive post-processing. The marginal creativity gain rarely justifies the variance cost.

## Interactions With Top-p and Top-k

Temperature does not operate in isolation. It interacts with top-p (nucleus sampling) and top-k, which filter the distribution before sampling.

Top-p sampling (nucleus sampling) restricts sampling to the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability mass reaches the configured threshold `p` — discarding the long tail beyond it. Combined with high temperature, this prevents the most extreme low-probability tokens from being selected while still allowing exploration within the reasonable probability space.

A common production pattern: temperature 0.7 with top-p 0.95. This combination produces varied outputs without venturing into highly unlikely token sequences that often correlate with errors or hallucinations.

Top-k sampling limits selection to the k most probable tokens. Combined with low temperature this is redundant — greedy decoding already handles it. Combined with high temperature, top-k prevents complete distribution flattening, maintaining quality guardrails.

The relationship between temperature and model size also matters. Larger models with better-calibrated distributions often need lower temperatures to achieve the same perceived creativity as smaller models — the larger model's probability distribution is more nuanced and its high-probability tokens are already more varied.

## Debugging Temperature-Related Issues

**Symptom:** Outputs nearly identical across runs despite expecting variation.
**Cause:** Temperature set too low (0.0–0.2) or greedy decoding by default.
**Fix:** Increase to 0.5–0.8 for most applications.

**Symptom:** Outputs become incoherent or contradict themselves mid-response.
**Cause:** Temperature above 1.5 — model sampling from low-quality probability regions.
**Fix:** Reduce to 0.7–1.0 and add top-p filtering.

**Symptom:** Model ignores specific instructions inconsistently.
**Cause:** High temperature allows exploration of low-probability paths that don't follow instructions.
**Fix:** Lower to 0.3–0.5 for instruction-following tasks, or improve prompt specificity.

**Symptom:** Creative outputs feel generic and repetitive.
**Cause:** Temperature too low (below 0.5) for a task that benefits from exploration.
**Fix:** Increase to 1.0–1.3. Prepare for increased output filtering need.
